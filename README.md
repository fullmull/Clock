<Hello World!>

It is usually the first sentence we write when learning to code. 
So, hello. Good day to you, the internet stranger reading this text right now. Sit tight, and listen for a moment.

Let this project serve as my digital memorial. Like you, I am a developer, bound by the same logic and speaking the same language. We understand each other, at least on a fundamental level.

Since I was young, I have been mesmerized by these machines operating purely on ones and zeros—a realm where you can create absolutely anything. You simply write what it should do, and it obeys. It feels like magic. Within this digital expanse, we fancy ourselves gods. Or, at least, we think we are. But in the waking world, even gods must bow to the laws of physics and, most cruelly, to time.

So, I built a time machine. I'm joking, of course; I built a clock. Just a simple clock that tells the time. I built it because no matter what you do, it will continue to tick, entirely unbothered by human striving. What about you? Do you know how much time you have left?
I certainly don't. Perhaps I am long gone by the time you stumble upon this repository. If so, make good use of it, since you are already here.

Let me anticipate the questions you might have:

Who am I, and what is my name? It matters little who I am or where I came from. I am merely a ghost in the machine, another stranger on the internet.
Why are there no comments in the code? I am certain you will figure it out; you need only dedicate a little time to it.
Is it free to use? I doubt I will have much use for copyrights in the grave. Do with it whatever you wish.
Why GitHub? I have always loved the ethos of free and open-source code. I wanted to leave something behind for you, and for myself. You receive a nice clock to use as you see fit, and I receive my digital memorial. Besides, this platform will likely outlive us all.

Now, a little about the project itself.

I named it the Orbit Clock. I have always harbored a deep fascination for planets, space, galaxies, and the solar system. Our home requires twenty-four hours to turn upon its axis before we can declare a day has passed, and three hundred and sixty-five days to circle the sun before we can call it a year. Long before digital screens, the very first watches were circular. The name, then, was far from random. That is why you will notice an orbital ring around the clockface—one that never stops turning. To ground the passing of time, it silently pulls from the galleries of Pixabay, painting the screen with landscapes and art of our earth—preserving the timeless beauty of a world I so admired.

I have provided a few customization options, though none of them will allow you to stop or rewind time. You can make it glow like our sun in the high sky, or perhaps select a green to remind you of living grass and ancient trees. You might choose the colors of a rainbow after a storm, a warm yellow like summer sand beneath your feet, or a deep blue for the sky and the endless seas. I really loved our home, this planet. Regardless, you have plenty of options to play with—and never enough time. 

May this clock tick long after my fingers have left the keys. Farewell to you.

In memorial to myself, an internet enthusiast of the twenty-first century.
The third planet of the Sol system—Earth.
